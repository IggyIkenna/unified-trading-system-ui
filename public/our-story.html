Our Story

I spent two and a half years trying to find the stack I needed. Nobody had it. So we built it.

Odum started as one founder's frustration with a fragmented vendor landscape. It grew into an institutional trading operating system that now runs across crypto, DeFi, traditional markets, sports, and prediction markets — and is offered to the firms that asked us for it.

Written by Ikenna Igboaka, Founder & CEO · LinkedIn

Part I — The problem

It started on a crypto arb desk.

I spent two and a half years running high-frequency cross-exchange arbitrage in crypto. It started extremely easy. It ended very hard. The market matured, the edges compressed, and the only way to stay competitive was to iterate on strategy faster than the market iterated on me.

The hard way is always the honest way to learn a lesson. Mine was this: without the ability to change your view of the world as fast as the market changes its view of you, you will run out of runway before you run out of ideas.

So I went on a solo mission to re-engineer the stack. Two goals: cheapen the cost base by stripping away the latency requirement, and build an arsenal of strategies rather than riding a single one.

Part II — The chicken-and-egg

Building a strategy is a chicken-and-egg problem.

You invest a lot of time to get one right, and it only makes sense if it makes money. If you already knew what was going to make money, you'd have stopped researching and started shipping. Everyone who has actually run a book knows this tension.

I needed a top-down view of the world. Not at a press-release level — at a medium-to-low level of resolution. I needed to be able to ask clean questions across the full universe of venues, instruments, and regimes, and get back signal that was comparable across domains. Only then could I decide where to spend research time next.

The question I kept asking myself: how do I build fast enough and cheap enough not to run out of money before it's too late?

Part III — The vendor search

I went looking — not for a strategy, for a stack.

Something that could give me the view of the universe I needed. Something I could build research on without throwing out the infrastructure every time the domain shifted. Over a long period I spoke to, trialled, or built proofs-of-concept across most of what exists. A partial list of what I evaluated:

  • Hummingbot — open-source market-making bots, tight scope, single domain
  • Amberdata, Tardis, Databento — excellent data, but data is one piece of the puzzle
  • Lean · QuantConnect — a real research stack, crypto-flavoured, limited venue reach
  • Deribit · IBKR · CoinRoutes — venue-specific execution, not cross-domain infrastructure
  • TradingView — charting and scripting, not an operating layer
  • Versify — high-quality ML execution, but the protocol assumed you sent entry, exit, take-profit and stop-loss for a single order at a time. Market-making wasn't a first-class citizen

Every one of them had a gap. Some were narrow by design. Some had deep strengths in one asset class and nothing of value in another — excellent sports arbitrage coverage and nothing usable in crypto, or the other way around. None of them were multi-dimensional enough for what I was trying to do.

Part IV — The Citadel realisation

I started to realise what I wanted simply didn't exist.

The only way I was going to get the stack I needed was to go to Citadel, Jane Street, or Two Sigma and convince them to let me build my pipeline inside theirs. They don't offer that as a product. It would be incredibly expensive if they did. And honestly, even they don't have crypto exchanges, traditional markets, sports, DeFi, and prediction markets unified under one stack at scale.

That's when the shape of the problem clarified. Every company has a gap. Every service provider has a gap. Even the best internal trading firms have gaps. If someone truly doesn't, they're not open to the public.

Why does the gap exist? Because to think multi-dimensionally enough to build infrastructure that serves the universe of strategy families across the universe of categories — while also having the experience to build institutional-grade trading software, security architecture, and latency optimisation — is an extremely rare combinatory skill set. Given my background and the way my brain is wired, it's the most exciting problem I could point myself at.

Part V — What we built

One operating system across five categories.

Odum is a unified trading infrastructure that treats CeFi, DeFi, traditional markets, sports, and prediction markets as a single taxonomy. One research layer. One execution layer. One risk layer. One reporting layer. The same code path in batch and live — the only seam that differs is whether a fill comes from a matching engine or a real venue.

The architectural discipline — a single source of truth for all data contracts, config-hashed versioning, shard-level failure isolation, honest-coverage manifests — is standard practice at top-tier firms. That alone puts the platform in an unusual category. What's genuinely novel is applying that discipline to the full spread: institutional-grade rigor on crypto exchange arbitrage, DeFi protocols across multiple chains, sports leagues, prediction markets, and traditional equities / futures / FX — inside one stack, with one strategy-instruction protocol, all traded by us.

The taxonomy

Five asset categories, one ontology. Every instrument, venue, timeframe and strategy archetype lives in the same registry. Cross-domain questions become comparable.

Batch equals live

The same component graph runs historical research and live trading. No separate backtest engine. No divergence between what you tested and what you shipped.

Institutional rigor, applied broadly

Contracts-first, config-hashed, failure-isolated. The quant-platform pattern you'd expect at a top-tier firm — extended to the domains those firms typically don't touch.

Part VI — The landscape

What the industry actually offers today.

It helps to see how the market is split.

On one side, there are signal-takers — execution firms, order-management systems. You design the strategy — say, buy bitcoin every time it drops 1% — hand over the instruction, and they route it to the venues. That service is genuinely useful. Venue access is harder than it looks: some exchanges require jurisdictional presence, others require regulatory permissions, and building infrastructure that trades crypto cleanly 24/7 over weekends is non-trivial work. Firms that specialise in keeping that stable exist in traditional markets and in crypto — but very few of them cover a serious cross-asset range.

On the other side, there are platforms that let you research and build strategies inside their infrastructure. This is a tiny niche. And it shrinks further the moment you ask for the full range — crypto, traditional markets, DeFi, sports, prediction — under one roof.

Odum is one of very few firms that does both — execution and research — and, as far as we can tell, the only one doing both across the full range.

Part VII — IP protection

Two ways to protect your IP.

Whenever the conversation moves to strategy, the first real question is about IP. It's a fair question. We built the product around it.

Execution-only firms exist because routing trades on your behalf isn't IP-sensitive — you are the broker's client; they do what you instruct. Handing over your actual research code is a very different conversation. DART — our platform — splits that conversation into two modes, and the right choice depends on where your edge lives.

DART · Signals In

Your code stays yours.

You keep your strategy in your own codebase. We never see the code and never see how you make money. What you get is the platform, reporting, regulatory coverage, data pipelines, and execution stack — the infrastructure pieces that are expensive to build from scratch. Your signal goes in; the rest of the pipeline does the job. You focus on what you're best at.

DART · Full

Build with us on a locked-off strategy.

For firms that want the interconnectedness of the full pipeline — data through research through execution through reporting through regulatory. A first call maps your needs; we match to the closest strategy in our catalogue and tailor it. Whatever we build together is locked off: it does not enter our investment-management book and we do not trade it ourselves. Exclusive to you for the length of the contract.

The catalogue sits behind the demo. It holds roughly 2,500 strategy combinations across the five categories, and we don't walk through it on day one. We get to the demo first, the first serious conversation, and then we open the door. That's not coy — it's how trust is built, and it protects the clients who have already locked off their piece.

Either way, we are not competing with you. The whole product is structured so we can't.

Part VIII — The math of 2,500 strategies

Why don't we just trade them all ourselves?

It's the obvious challenge. You've seen the catalogue — raise a few billion and run them all. I've thought about it. The numbers don't work.

We do trade our catalogue ourselves. Week by week we take strategies from cold through lukewarm to live — the final rounds of live-capital validation. But the catalogue is a catalogue precisely because the infrastructure makes it possible, not because we've personally traded every combination. Even at a two-week pace per strategy — faster than anything I've seen in production — 2,500 strategies is 5,000 weeks. A hundred years of work. That's before you add the team, the sales force, and the operational capacity to raise and run capital against each one.

Partnership is how the math works. If a hundred partners each run $100M of Odum-backed strategies, we reach $10B under management without raising another pound — and every partner is an extension of ourselves. It's a genuine affiliation, aligned through revenue-share. When partners grow, we grow. That's the dual incentive, and it's load-bearing.

Part IX — On strategy decay

Strategies decay. The accelerator around them is what matters.

Anyone who has actually traded knows that strategies decay. That's a fact of the market, not a flaw in the firm. What matters is the rate at which you can iterate, and the platform around each strategy.

Every strategy on the platform starts with a baseline, moves through backtest, paper-traded validation, and eventually live-traded results. Strategies evolve — v1, v2, v3 — the same way any serious trader evolves their book. Clients can and should evolve theirs. The theoretical cap on strategy count across the taxonomy is vast, and we're nowhere near it.
