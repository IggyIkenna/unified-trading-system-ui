Our Story

I spent two and a half years trying to find the stack I needed. Nobody had it. So we built it.

Odum started as one founder's frustration with a fragmented vendor landscape. It grew into an institutional trading operating system that now runs across crypto, DeFi, traditional markets, sports, and prediction markets — and is offered to the firms that asked us for it.

Written by Ikenna Igboaka, Founder & CEO · LinkedIn

Part I — The problem

It started on a crypto arb desk.

I spent two and a half years running high-frequency cross-exchange arbitrage in crypto. It started extremely easy. It ended very hard. The market matured, the edges compressed, and the only way to stay competitive was to iterate on strategy faster than the market iterated on me.

The hard way is always the honest way to learn a lesson. Mine was this: without the ability to change your view of the world as fast as the market changes its view of you, you will run out of runway before you run out of ideas.

So I went on a solo mission to re-engineer the stack. Two goals: reduce sole reliance on latency — compete on smarts, not just speed — and build an arsenal of strategies rather than riding a single one.

Part II — The chicken-and-egg

Building a strategy is a chicken-and-egg problem.

You invest a lot of time to get one right, and it only makes sense if it makes money. If you already knew what was going to make money, you'd have stopped researching and started shipping. Everyone who has actually run a book knows this tension.

I needed a top-down view of the world. Not at a press-release level — at a medium-to-low level of resolution. I needed to be able to ask clean questions across the full universe of venues, instruments, and regimes, and get back signal that was comparable across domains. Only then could I decide where to spend research time next.

The question I kept asking myself: how do I build fast enough and cheap enough not to run out of money before it's too late?

Part III — The vendor search

I went looking — not for a strategy, for a stack.

Something that could give me the view of the universe I needed. Something I could build research on without throwing out the infrastructure every time the domain shifted. Over a long period I spoke to, trialled, or built proofs-of-concept across most of what exists. A partial list — each of them strong at what they set out to do:

  • Hummingbot — best-in-class open-source market-making framework. Deliberately tight on scope so the bots stay simple and predictable.
  • Amberdata, Tardis, Databento — three of the cleanest data providers in crypto and beyond. We still source from firms in this tier. Data is one piece of the puzzle; I needed research and execution layers on top of it.
  • Lean · QuantConnect — a genuine research stack with a strong community. Crypto-flavoured, and the venue / asset breadth I needed sat outside its focus.
  • Deribit · IBKR · CoinRoutes — venue-specific execution that does its job well. None of them was trying to be cross-domain infrastructure, and rightly so.
  • TradingView — the best charting and scripting UX in the business. We still use it for some of our charts today. It was never trying to be an operating layer.
  • Versify — high-quality ML execution. The protocol assumes single-order instructions with entry / exit / take-profit / stop-loss — the right shape for their customer, not the shape I needed to run market-making through the same pipeline.

To be clear: none of this is a criticism. Several of these firms do what they do better than anyone else, and some of them cover ground we don’t (and don’t need to). We still use TradingView for charting, we still source from the data-provider tier, and the story here isn’t that they fell short — it’s that the combined problem I was trying to solve needed a shape none of them was built for on its own.

So the instinct isn’t to compete with them; it’s to partner. We actively welcome partnerships with trading companies, hedge funds, fund admins, exchanges, and analytics / pricing platforms — anyone whose capability complements ours rather than overlaps. Odum has a unique combined product; several of the firms above have genuinely great ones of their own. When complementary platforms cooperate, everyone’s clients win. Our job is to make the combined offering better, not to pretend we’re the only room in the building.

Part IV — The Citadel realisation

I started to realise what I wanted simply didn't exist.

The only way I was going to get the stack I needed was to go to Citadel, Jane Street, or Two Sigma and convince them to let me build my pipeline inside theirs. They don't offer that as a product. It would be incredibly expensive if they did. And honestly, even they don't have crypto exchanges, traditional markets, sports, DeFi, and prediction markets unified under one stack at scale.

That's when the shape of the problem clarified. Every company has a gap. Every service provider has a gap. Even the best internal trading firms have gaps. If someone truly doesn't, they're not open to the public.

Why does the gap exist? Because to think multi-dimensionally enough to build infrastructure that serves the universe of strategy families across the universe of categories — while also having the experience to build institutional-grade trading software, security architecture, and latency optimisation — is an extremely rare combinatory skill set. Given my background and the way my brain is wired, it's the most exciting problem I could point myself at.

Part V — What we built

One operating system across five categories.

Odum is a unified trading infrastructure that treats CeFi, DeFi, traditional markets, sports, and prediction markets as a single taxonomy. One research layer. One execution layer. One risk layer. One reporting layer. Simulation equals live — the same code path runs historical replay and live trading; the only seam that differs is whether a fill comes from a matching engine or a real venue.

The architectural discipline — a single source of truth for all data contracts, config-hashed versioning, shard-level failure isolation, honest-coverage manifests — is standard practice at top-tier firms. That alone puts the platform in an unusual category. What's genuinely novel is applying that discipline to the full spread: institutional-grade rigor on crypto exchange arbitrage, DeFi protocols across multiple chains, sports leagues, prediction markets, and traditional equities / futures / FX — inside one stack, with one strategy-instruction protocol, all traded by us.

The taxonomy

Five asset categories, one ontology. Every instrument, venue, timeframe and strategy archetype lives in the same registry. Cross-domain questions become comparable.

Batch equals live

The same component graph runs historical research and live trading. No separate backtest engine. No divergence between what you tested and what you shipped.

Institutional rigor, applied broadly

Contracts-first, config-hashed, failure-isolated. The quant-platform pattern you'd expect at a top-tier firm — extended to the domains those firms typically don't touch.

Part VI — The landscape

What the industry actually offers today.

It helps to see how the market is split.

On one side, there are signal-takers — execution firms, order-management systems. You design the strategy — say, buy bitcoin every time it drops 1% — hand over the instruction, and they route it to the venues. That service is genuinely useful. Venue access is harder than it looks: some exchanges require jurisdictional presence, others require regulatory permissions, and building infrastructure that trades crypto cleanly 24/7 over weekends is non-trivial work. Firms that specialise in keeping that stable exist in traditional markets and in crypto — but very few of them cover a serious cross-asset range.

On the other side, there are platforms that let you research and build strategies inside their infrastructure. This is a tiny niche. And it shrinks further the moment you ask for the full range — crypto, traditional markets, DeFi, sports, prediction — under one roof.

Odum is one of very few firms that does both — execution and research — and, as far as we can tell, the only one doing both across the full range.
